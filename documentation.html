The pre-1800 theme is based on western illuminated manuscripts from 700-1600 C.E. Layouts and styles vary throughout this period based on production location, use, and date. The CSS theme is a hybrid of some of the most distinguishable features of western manuscripts, particularly in Latin, throughout this period.

Layout

The page is laid out in a single unbroken column. I selected a one-column layout for the style because it is very common in medieval manuscripts and easier to read with three articles displayed simultaneously in the website.

Text is left-aligned, standard practice in western manuscripts. Although the medieval page is ruled, the text is not justified. I took particular inspiration from the Lindisfarne Gospels which do not fill white space at the end of the line and often eschews hyphenation. The text is also inline, with no breaks between paragraphs and even subheadings, as is typical in medieval manuscripts. I have retained punctuation for modern readability.

Margins vary in manuscripts due to time period, location, use, and other factors. Mathematical principles for page layout and margins were published throughout the period but were not universally adopted. The CSS layout is inspired by a common layout in which the right margin is approximately twice the size of the upper margin, and the lower margin is approximately double the outside. The choice to use no left margin or gutter space is taken from richly illuminated Books of Hours from the high Middle Ages and lack of necessity for page gutters on a webpage.

Script

The script used is "Uncial Antiqua" which combines letter forms from uncial and half uncial. Uncial is a majuscule script commonly used in the early Middle Ages. Closely related to these scripts is insular script (also called insular half-uncial), which originated in Ireland and is used in the Book of Kells, the Lindisfarne Gospels, and the Book of Durrow.

I selected an uncial script because they are relatively easy to read, especially when compared to later Gothic scripts. "Uncial Antiqua" was a faithful digital font interpretation I found.

Color

Text color:

By far the most common color for text is black, followed by red then blue. Rubrication was often used in manuscripts for titles, subtitles, incipits, explicits and other purposes. The CSS style uses red for subheadings and the selected metadata words. I chose to make the main titles blue since oftentimes, especially in Bibles, the names of the books are given in blue, red, or some combination plus the medieval page generally has a variety of colors to help the reader navigate the page at a glance. With so much red in the subheadings and metadata selections, I thought a bit of blue would help readers distinguish the title. In both cases, the red and blue used in the CSS is slightly darker than what would be found in a manuscript since the bright red and bright blue were uncomfortable for the eye when lit with the computer screen.

Cursor

The cursor recalls medieval manicula, drawings of a pointing finger in the margin added by the reader and/or scribe to emphasize certain passages and help the reader navigate the page. I retained the default white pointing hand for clickable elements to encourage the user to reflect on the nature of text, technology, and how we relate to it across time. Reflection on the nature of time and one's place in the world of God's creation is essential to the experience of an illuminated medieval manuscript.

This style is inspired by the layouts of posters and magazines published during the Art Nouveau movement. This artistic movement flourished throughout Europe and the United States during the 1890s with maximum diffusion during the last period of the so-called Belle Époque, just before the outbreak of the First World War in 1914. It covered almost every art field, from architecture to decorative arts, book illustrations and more. Art Nouveau artists, inspired by plant forms and nature, took organic subjects, and flattened and abstracted them into sophisticated, sinuous, and flowing motifs. Asymmetrical shapes, female forms and exotic woods were also recurrent.

Ver Sacrum, founded by Gustav Klimt and Max Kurzweil in 1898, was the official magazine of the Vienna Secession and redefined the canons of editorial graphics by unifying its elements, including advertisements.

Advertisement posters in style Art Nouveau at the end of the January 1898 volume (Source).

Layout

Ver Sacrum utilised a unique square format which offered new possibilities in layout for the designer’s use of multiple text columns and decorative borders. Ver Sacrum in its publications uses a double-column as well as one-column layout: I opted for a single column to guarantee to the user a good readability while the three articles are displayed at once.

The alignment of the text is justified, the first letter of the first paragraph is usually decorated while the first line is indented. Though at the beginning they opted for capital letters decorated with natural hornaments or humans in nature (see picture below) in some paragraphs of the magazines, from 1900 on they decided for a capital letter without peculiar decorations. You can see the font that inspired my choice below. At first I opted for a floral capital letter; then I decided to use a simpler option for the capital letter in order to represent better the style Ver Sacrum had at the beginning of the 20th century.

Due to its square format, Ver Sacrum margins were very wide. I decided to not use a very large value for the margins of the elements inside the articles’ containers since the space in the viewport is quite limited, anyway it can be notices that the margins of “Art Nouveau” CSS style are bigger than the ones of the other styles.

Sometimes, to fill the page, some patterns were placed right below the ending of the last paragraph. In the Art Nouveau stylesheet you can find some figures at the end of each metadata and article column.

Abstract figures were used to fill the page (Source).

Script

Edda is the font used for titles, subtitles (inspired by the figures below) and for the starting capital letters. Art Nouveau magazines normally chose serif fonts for the paragraphs, as to follow the synuosity of the natural decorations: Tarocco Roman – a serif script – was selected for the paragraphs. At that time sans serif fonts existed but were not widely used. Only later, in 1920, the Bauhaus popularised sans serif as a reaction to the embellished Art Nouveau style.

Edda seemed perfect for the titles since it immediately recalls the Art Nouveau style. Before I experimented with Liberty font for the titles (a similar font was used in the Italian magazine Emporium) but it did not represent the style as well as Edda in my opinion.

Images and colours

Ver Sacrum and other Art Nouveau magazines contain different kind of art: pictorial art, as decorations, drawings and sketches and more - not only black and white - and photographs - mostly black and white. For the multimedia content of the articles I decided to add a black and white filter in order to mime how Ver Sacrum attached photographs in their magazines.

This solution was chosen even for the Art Nouveau stylesheet in this online magazine, but, instead of placing the caption on the side of the image, it was preferred to leave it under the figure, in order to keep a decent size for the picture and to mantain a readable font size for the caption.

Example of a figure caption (Source).

The colours used in Ver Sacrum magazine are few, and mostly used in the decorations, sketches or for some titles. Art Nouveau posters are known for their warm colours, such as different shades of orange, yellow and pale pink. Art Nouveau artists used colours wisely, combining the main color with its respective secondary color, or with others to find a perfect harmony, as can be noticed in the Mucha's decorative patterns and litography above. For the colours of the CSS style I decided to use different shades of khaki yellow and pale pink, to match the two decorations used in the background. The links in the page are pale pink too.

Layout

Futuristic style is inspired by an ideal world in which nature wins. Technologies and nature coexist in a harmonious world where the human being learnt to preserve natural landscapes, even with the digital applications and software’s help. The idea of the two worlds (human-dependent technologies and wild nature) in a reciprocal relationship of coexistence is represented by the overlapping of different columns containing the articles to a majestic uncontaminated natural environment (Norwegian fjords).

This was another option for the background.

The overlapping elements are almost transparent to let the user appreciate the view: will the next decade’s generations be able to appreciate such natural landscapes in the real world? The 2030 style will not answer to this question, leaving it open to reflect on it.

The user of the future will probably read and compare the articles through hololenses. The imaginary device understands and reads the user’s eye movement and follows it, so once it understands the user is looking at a certain article, it activates, this means it is no more transparent, but its content is revealed. This should facilitate the 2030 users to keep concentrate and focused, erasing possibly distracting content from the sight. If the device understands the user needs to read from the three articles at the same time in order to confront some specific parts, the transparency is deactivated to let him compare them (this is an imaginary feature, not implemented in the prototype).

Fonts

Minimalism and simplicity are keys of this style. The fonts used are all sans serif as, in my opinion, serif fonts will not be used in 2030 as designers will opt for easy-to-read and clean writings.

Colours

The colours chosen for the columns of the articles belong to the same colour palette of the image used in the background. I decided to not use high contrasts in the layout of the page to not strain the eyes and I opted for a grey text over dark background for the same reason. The titles and the links are brighter to capture the attention of the reader. Also the filter for images is specifically created to desaturate them in order to let them display harmoniously with the background and not strain the eyes.

Accessibility

Also, the text inside of the paragraphs is aligned on the left and not justified, because the justification could cause some reading problems to people affected by dyslexia, as it creates large uneven spaces between letters and words.
